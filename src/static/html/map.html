Mining in different countries

Сhina is by far the most preferred country for crypto miners. It is followed by the Czech Republic, Iceland, Japan, Georgia, and Russia.

According to Blockchain info data, which lists the world’s largest mining pools, we can conclude that 81% of the mining power is concentrated in China. Quite impressive, right?

Bitcoin mining has become a serious issue in terms of waste and pollution, which is why eco-friendly mining has become a positive trend. According to Digiconomist data, as of May 2018, the total energy consumed for Bitcoin mining calculated in terawatt hours (TW/h) was 64.36TW/h in annual terms.

Iceland is another country favored by Bitcoin and cryptocurrency miners, and it’s not only because of its relatively cheap energy – the country boasts hydroelectric power stations that provide clean energy. Also you don’t need to pay for conditioning.

Interestingly, the global energy consumption for Bitcoin mining surpasses the individual consumption of more than 160 countries, with New Zealand, Serbia, and Iceland among them. That is a huge demand for energy, and if you think about the fact that most of the mining energy comes from China’s coal-based electricity, the situation is worrying.

Mining problems

PwC blockchain analyst Alex de Vries said in an interview to Bloomberg: The energy consumption is insane. If we start using this on a global scale, it will kill the planet.

Thus, the price of mining crypto is not the only factor that miners should consider when thinking about where to settle. We should start thinking about the environment as well. Iceland is the obvious winner here, but the problem is that it is a small country and cannot supply more large-scale energy.

In February, Johann Snorri Sigurbergsson of Iceland’s energy firm HS Orka said that the country’s Bitcoin mining alone would account for 840 gigawatt-hours of electricity per year, exceeding the citizens’ consumption at 700 gigawatt-hours per year.

Significance of trading price

If you are looking to make profit from mining Bitcoin, its trading price is always something to think about, especially when the Bitcoin price has moved through a market correction and is currently fluctuating around $9,200. In April 2018, Morgan Stanley equity analyst Charlie Chan stated that mining Bitcoin is not profitable when its price is below $8,600.

“We estimated the break-even point for big mining pools should be US$8,600, even if we assume a very low electricity cost (US$0.03 kW/h),” Chan said.

Despite the fact that the cryptocurrency market is fluctuating only at about 50% of its record high, the Bitcoin rush shows no sign of slowing down, and the countries offering cheap electricity are set to be the biggest winners.
